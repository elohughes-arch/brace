The old way

The day was always the easy part. Writing it up was not.

You come in off the last drive — boots wet, dogs tired, the light going. The day deserves a record: the estate, the drives, the bag by species, the wind, the one shot you'll talk about for years.

But the notebook stays in the Land Rover. Memory blurs by the second peg. Weeks later the season is a handful of half-remembered dates and a few photographs with no story attached. The traditional game book is a beautiful thing — and a quiet burden.

Brace removes the desk.

The notebook that stays empty

Good intentions rarely survive a long day in the field. Most days go unrecorded — and memory does the rest.

How it works

Three steps. You only do the first one.

From the field to the page, with nothing in between for you to do.

01

Wear the camera and shoot the day

Clip your POV or action camera to your cap, vest or stock and shoot as you always have. That's the whole of your part — no app to fumble between drives, no buttons to press as a bird comes over.

02

Brace reads it and finds what mattered

Once you're home, Brace quietly works through your footage and picks out the key moments — the shots, the rhythm of each drive, the conditions and the ground you covered — so you never scrub through hours to find the day.

03

Your day is written into your game book

Those moments become a proper entry: the date and estate, the drives in order, the bag by species, the weather and light, the guns in the line. You arrive to a finished page, not a blank one.

The Game Book

The record a shoot has always kept — now it keeps itself.

For generations, the game book has been the truest record of a sporting life — the date, the moor or estate, each drive, the bag by species, the conditions, and the moments worth remembering. Brace carries that tradition forward and writes it for you.
